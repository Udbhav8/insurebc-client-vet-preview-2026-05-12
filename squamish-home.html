Coverage by how you use the property

How you live in or rent out a Sea-to-Sky property changes which policy form fits. Get this wrong and a claim can get denied.

Primary residence - Standard homeowners policy with full contents, liability, and additional living expense. The lowest-premium category.
Vacation or second home - Occupancy gaps change the rate. Higher water-damage deductibles, freeze-up clauses if heat is off, and vacancy permits after extended absence.
Short-term rental (Airbnb/VRBO) - Most home policies exclude commercial use. Needs a specific STR endorsement or commercial landlord policy. Required for BC STR Act compliance.
Long-term rental - Rental dwelling policy with landlord liability and loss of rental income. Tenant's own contents are not your responsibility.

Coast Mountains hazards your policy should address

The Sea-to-Sky corridor has a hazard profile most BC home policies were not written for. We make sure the right endorsements are in place before they are needed.

Wildfire interface - Brackendale, Cheekye, Britannia Beach, and Coast Mountains backcountry properties sit in the wildland-urban interface. FireSmart practices and metal roofing keep carriers on risk.
Debris flow & landslide - Cheekye fan, Mamquam Blind Channel, and historical slide paths are excluded from most standard policies. Hazard review required for high-exposure parcels.
Atmospheric river flooding - Overland water endorsement recommended for low-lying Squamish, Brackendale, and Pemberton properties after the 2021 Sumas-style events.
Earthquake (Cascadia) - The corridor is in a high-risk seismic zone. Earthquake is excluded by default; endorsement runs $200-$800/year with a 10-20% deductible.

Squamish & Whistler short-term rental insurance

Squamish and Whistler combined are one of Canada's largest short-term rental markets by share of housing stock. Standard home and condo policies exclude commercial use, so an Airbnb or VRBO booking without the right endorsement can void coverage on a single fire or theft claim.

We work with carriers that endorse short-term rental specifically (Aviva, Wawanesa, Intact specialty) and arrange commercial landlord policies for hosts running multiple units. FAQ

How much is home insurance in Squamish or Whistler?
A detached Squamish single-family home typically runs $1,400-$2,800/year. A Whistler ski-in/out vacation condo runs $900-$2,400/year (often with a rental endorsement), and a 4-6 bedroom Whistler chalet runs $3,000-$8,000/year. Pemberton rural acreage with outbuildings tends to land in the $1,800-$3,500/year range. Wildfire interface, distance to a hydrant, replacement cost, and earthquake endorsement all move the number.
Does home insurance cover Airbnb in Whistler?
Almost never under a standard policy. Most home and condo policies exclude short-term rental (under 30-day) use, so renting on Airbnb or VRBO without telling your insurer can void coverage. We work with carriers that offer specific short-term rental endorsements or commercial-style landlord policies (Aviva, Wawanesa, Intact specialty). If you host in Whistler or Squamish you need to disclose it and add the right endorsement.
Do I need earthquake insurance in the Sea-to-Sky?
Strongly recommended. The corridor sits on the Cascadia subduction zone and modeled losses from a major event are significant. Earthquake is not included in standard home policies in BC and must be added as an endorsement. Premium is typically $200-$800/year depending on property type and deductible (commonly 10-20%).
Is wildfire covered by home insurance in Squamish?
Yes, fire including wildfire is a standard insured peril on most home policies. What changes in the Sea-to-Sky is underwriting appetite for properties in the wildland-urban interface (Brackendale, Cheekye, Britannia interface, Coast Mountains backcountry). FireSmart yard work, defensible space, and metal roofing all help carriers stay on risk. We place homes other carriers decline.
What is the difference between a vacation home and primary residence policy?
A primary residence policy assumes the home is occupied most of the year. A vacation or seasonal home policy reflects occupancy gaps, which usually means higher water-damage deductibles, freeze-up exclusions if the heat is turned off, and sometimes vacancy permit requirements after 30-60 days unoccupied. Whistler vacation properties also need to disclose whether they are rented out and on what terms.
Does my policy cover debris flow or landslide in the corridor?
Landslide and debris flow are excluded from most standard home policies in BC. Properties on the Cheekye fan, near the Mamquam Blind Channel, or below known historical slide paths in the Sea-to-Sky should request a hazard review. Some carriers offer limited overland water or earth movement coverage. For high-exposure properties, mortgage lenders may require specific endorsements.
